01 · Design
    

What's a robot actually made of?

    

Every robot — from a tiny line-follower to a full-size humanoid — is built from the same five ingredients. Mix them differently and you get a totally different machine.

  
  
    
🦾

Chassis

The body and skeleton. It holds every other part together and decides the robot's shape — wheels, legs, or tracks.

    
👁️

Sensors

The robot's senses. Cameras, distance sensors, and microphones let it notice walls, light, sound, and objects around it.

    
🧠

Controller

The brain. A small computer (often a Raspberry Pi or microcontroller) reads the sensors and decides what to do next.

    
⚙️

Actuators

The muscles. Motors and servos turn electrical signals into real movement — spinning wheels, gripping claws, walking legs.

    
🔋

Power

The energy source. Batteries keep everything running — better batteries mean a robot can work longer without a recharge.

    
📐

Design loop

Real robot builders sketch, prototype, test, and redesign again and again — failure is just part of getting it right.

04 · Useful info
    

Good-to-know facts

    

Tap a card to open it.

  
  
    What's the difference between a robot and a regular machine?     

A machine like a toaster only does one fixed action. A robot senses its surroundings, makes a decision, and then acts — it can change its behavior based on what it notices, like a robot vacuum choosing a new path around a chair.

  
  
    What is "AI" doing inside a robot?     

AI is the decision-making software. Sensors gather information, and AI helps the robot figure out what that information means and what to do next — like recognizing a face or planning a route across a room.

  
  
    Do I need to know math to build robots?     

A little goes a long way. Angles help with turning, basic geometry helps with movement, and simple logic (if this, then that) is the heart of almost every robot program.

  
  
    What's the easiest way to start at home or school?     

Block-based coding platforms let you snap instructions together like puzzle pieces instead of typing code — it's the same starting point most professional roboticists used too.

  
  
    What competitions can school students join?     

Programs like FIRST Robotics and RoboCup Junior let student teams design, build, and program real robots to complete challenges against other schools.

  
  
    
05 · Latest updates
    

How robotics is evolving right now

    

A quick, kid-friendly snapshot of where the field stands in 2026.

  
  
    
      
In classrooms
      

Robots are showing up as helpers, not replacements

      

Schools around the world are trying out classroom robots like NAO, Pepper, and the more affordable Alpha Mini to help teach coding and even practice languages. Every study agrees on one thing: robots work best alongside a real teacher, not instead of one.

    
    
      
Getting cheaper
      

Robots are becoming affordable enough for families

      

A few years ago, a walking, dancing humanoid robot cost as much as a car. New entry-level models now sell for close to the price of a good laptop, with simple drag-and-drop coding built in for beginners.

    
    
      
Smarter brains
      

Robots are starting to learn by watching

      

Instead of being programmed move-by-move, some new robots learn tasks by watching a person do them first, then practicing until they get it right — much like how you might learn a new skill by watching a video first.

    
    
      
Big moment
      

Humanoid robots took over the stage at CES 2026

      

This year's big tech show was packed with walking, talking humanoid robots from companies all over the world, showing off skills from folding laundry to running at a jog.

    
    
      
Why it matters for students
      

The fundamentals still come first

      

Educators keep repeating the same advice: before building a fancy humanoid, learn the basics — logical thinking, simple code, and hands-on building with beginner kits. Those skills carry over to any robot you'll ever work with.
